I Needed You

By Peck  ·  Combat Medic  ·  CVICU  ·  Chiari Patient

Follow Along

I took a leap of faith. I was the most vulnerable I've ever been online. I showed every ounce of myself — and the whole time, I couldn't stop thinking about who was watching.

People from church. My parents' friends. Friends from the military. Friends I've known since I was a kid. Would they think I'm weak? Would they think I'm slow? It's hard to lay all your cards on the table. It's hard to admit you don't have it together. These are not easy things to do.

But today — my community showed up and showed out.

Childhood friends. Military brothers and sisters. My sister's friends. My parents' friends — people they've known since they were kids. They all came. And they all said the same thing: We're praying. We got you.

Being the Patient

I'll be real with you. Getting help is not something I want to do. I don't want to be seen as weak. I'll run into a burning building. I'll save a cat from a tree. But being the patient? That's not a role I want. I want to be the one treating. I want to be the one serving.

But here I am.

"My grace is sufficient for you, for my power is made perfect in weakness."

— 2 Corinthians 12:9

I used to say that verse. I've quoted it to patients. I've written it in cards. But I never thought I'd be the one who needed it said back to me. And here's what I'm realizing — maybe you can't give what you haven't received. Maybe that's been the point all along.

This — right here, these words — this is my outlet. This is how I love people right now.

Nobody Caught It

I was diagnosed with ADHD at a young age. My whole life, I had problems I couldn't put a finger on. My parents did everything right. Doctor's visit after doctor's visit. We went through every step trying to figure out what was wrong.

But nobody caught it.

That makes me ask — should an MRI be standard? How many people are out there misdiagnosed with ADHD, or vertigo, or something else entirely, when what they actually need is someone to walk in with the scan results and say: we found something?

I was diagnosed with vertigo. That's not what I have. I have a Chiari malformation. And I'm having brain surgery in five days.

People deserve the second doctor. People deserve the truth.

I needed someone to stand at my bedside and tell me I wasn't making it up. I needed someone to treat me like a person — not like an appliance attached to a hospital bed in a hallway. I spent 40 hours on that bed in a hallway. And I want to be clear — it's not the nurses' fault. Our medical system is severely depleted. The resources our staff have right now are nowhere near what they need.

I had a neurosurgeon tell me it wasn't that bad. I had another one walk in and give me the gravity of my situation straight. I needed that second doctor. So does someone else right now.

The Prayer Is Being Answered

Last spring, while I was still working in the medical field, I made a simple prayer — that God would give me the ability to be more empathetic toward my patients. I think He's answering that prayer right now.

Paul said he would boast in his weaknesses so that the power of Christ could rest on him. I don't want to waste this weakness either. These next two months that I'm down, that I can't do much — maybe this is the time. Maybe I can encourage others. Show people they're not alone. Talk to veterans. Meet people who need someone to be vulnerable and say: hey, it's okay that you don't have it all together.

Maybe this is the gift.

We can sit here and talk about the 22 veterans who take their lives every day. We can talk about depression. We can talk about patients who feel something is wrong and get dismissed anyway. Or we can do something about it.

There is no part of me that wants to waste this.

What I'm Actually Asking For

People keep asking what they can do. What do I need? The medic in me keeps redirecting — what can I do for others?

I don't want meals. I don't want anything during surgery. What I want is simple: share this. Send it to one person who needs it. The one whose hands are tingling. The one who's been told it's not that bad by someone who doesn't have to live in their body. The one who feels like something is wrong and can't get anyone to listen.

That person exists. You know them. Send it.

And if you want to follow along — get on the email list or check back here. Surgery is February 26th. I'll be on the other side of it soon. And I'll have a lot to say.

I needed you — and you showed up.
Now let's show up for someone else.
Let's get to work.
